Week 1

A working idea for my final project

When I first began in neuroscience, I was very interested in the idea of memory, specifically how it degenerates in Alzheimer's Disease. That interest has never gone away. I was first an NSF Fellow studying biomarkers for Alzheimer's at the Yassa Lab, and most recently I studied decision-making at MIT's Hamilos Lab. All of this drove me to ask the question: how can we best interface with the brain to 1) understand memory and 2) treat neurodegenerative disease?

With this goal in mind, I began as an intern at Nia Therapeutics, a brain–computer interface (BCI) company developing a therapeutic for memory loss. Now, in PHYSCI70, I want to ask a related question: how can I build a simplified design that gives us a glimpse into the brain?

One major inspiration for this project was the House–Tennis Test , in which a patient in a vegetative state can communicate by answering questions through imagining either a house or playing tennis. Because these tasks activate different parts of the brain, the interviewer can infer a yes or no response.

My idea is to use an accessible EEG to read brain waves. First, the interviewer asks a question (such as “Do you like ice cream?”). If the answer is yes, the user is asked to solve a math problem, producing beta waves associated with focus. If the answer is no, the user relaxes, producing alpha waves associated with rest. These signals would trigger a small circuit that moves a dial to indicate “Yes” or “No.”

I recognize that this is an ambitious project. Alternative ideas along the same theme include a device that visualizes memory decay through fading LEDs, or a lamp that responds to heart rate as a speculative indicator of emotional state.
